How a Camera Works

CAM-101  ·  Week 1 reference

What a camera is doing

Light travels through four decision points on its way to becoming a photograph.
Light
The world reflects light. Your camera collects it.
Aperture
How wide the lens opens. Controls brightness and depth of field.
Shutter
How long it stays open. Controls time -- freezes or blurs motion.
Film / ISO
How sensitive the film is. Set when you load -- fixed for the roll.
Image
Light becomes a latent image on the film. Made permanent in the darkroom.
Every photograph is the result of three decisions: how wide (aperture), how long (shutter), and how sensitive (ISO). Change one and the others are affected. This is the exposure triangle.

Shooting modes

Not all cameras are fully manual. Here is what the mode dial means.
M
Manual
You set both aperture and shutter speed. The meter tells you if you are balanced. You decide whether to listen to it.
All cameras in this course are used in Manual mode.
Av
Aperture Priority
You set the aperture. The camera sets the shutter speed to match the light. Use when depth of field is your priority.
Good for portraits and landscapes where focus range matters most.
Tv
Shutter Priority
You set the shutter speed. The camera sets the aperture. Use when freezing or blurring motion is your priority.
Good for sports, action, or intentional blur.
P
Program / Auto
The camera sets everything. Not used in this course -- it removes the decisions that make photography expressive.
You will not use this mode this semester.

This course uses one film stock all semester: Kodak Tri-X 400, rated at ISO 400, developed in Kodak D-76 diluted 1:1 at 20°C for 10 minutes. Standardizing on one film removes variables so you can clearly see cause and effect in your exposures.
